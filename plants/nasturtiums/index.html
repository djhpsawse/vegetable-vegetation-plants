How to Grow Nasturtiums

A practical guide for colourful, edible wild-style flowers

About nasturtiums

Nasturtiums (Tropaeolum majus) are easy-to-grow annual flowers with vibrant blooms and round, shield-like leaves. They’re often used in cottage gardens and wildflower-style plantings because they thrive in poorer soils, attract pollinators, and their flowers and leaves are edible with a peppery flavour.

Choosing the right site

  • Light: Full sun to light partial shade. More sun = more flowers.
  • Soil: Well-drained, not too rich. Overly fertile soil encourages leaves over flowers.
  • Shelter: A spot protected from strong winds is ideal for trailing varieties.
Tip: Nasturtiums are great for borders, pots, hanging baskets, and as a “wild” edge along paths or vegetable beds.

Choosing varieties

There are two main growth habits to consider:

  • Bush (compact) nasturtiums: Form low mounds, perfect for beds and containers.
  • Trailing/climbing nasturtiums: Produce long stems that can spill over walls, climb supports, or cover bare ground.

Flower colours range from yellow and orange to deep red and cream, often with attractive markings. Choose a mix for a wildflower-style look.

Sowing nasturtium seeds

When to sow

  • Indoors: 4–6 weeks before your last expected frost.
  • Outdoors: After all danger of frost has passed and soil has warmed.

Sowing outdoors (direct in the ground)

  1. Prepare the soil: Loosen the top 15–20 cm and remove large stones and weeds.
  2. Spacing: Sow seeds about 2 cm deep, 25–30 cm apart for bush types, 30–45 cm for trailing types.
  3. Water: Water the area gently after sowing to settle the soil around the seeds.
  4. Germination: Seeds usually sprout in 7–14 days in warm conditions.

Sowing in pots or trays

  1. Containers: Use small pots or modules with drainage holes.
  2. Compost: Fill with a general-purpose potting mix; avoid heavy feeding.
  3. Depth: Sow one seed per cell or pot, about 2 cm deep.
  4. Care: Keep compost just moist, not waterlogged, and place in a bright, frost-free spot.
  5. Transplanting: Once seedlings have 2–3 true leaves and frost risk has passed, harden off and plant outside.

Looking after nasturtiums

Watering

  • New plants: Keep soil evenly moist until established.
  • Established plants: Water during dry spells, but allow the surface to dry between waterings.

Feeding

Nasturtiums generally don’t need extra fertiliser. Too much feed encourages lush foliage and fewer flowers. In containers, a very light, occasional feed is enough if plants look pale.

Training and support

  • Bush types: No support needed; they form neat mounds.
  • Trailing/climbing types: Provide a low trellis, fence, or allow them to trail from baskets and raised beds.

Companion planting

Nasturtiums are often grown near vegetables as a companion plant. They attract pollinators and can act as a “trap crop” for pests like aphids, drawing them away from more valuable plants.

Common problems and how to handle them

Aphids

Aphids love nasturtiums. If colonies build up, you can:

  • Wash off: Spray with a firm jet of water.
  • Hand removal: Pinch off heavily infested stems or leaves.
  • Encourage predators: Ladybirds and hoverflies help keep aphids in check.

Leaf problems

  • Yellowing leaves: Often due to overwatering or poor drainage. Let soil dry slightly and improve drainage.
  • Holes in leaves: Can be caused by caterpillars or slugs. Check plants regularly and remove pests by hand where possible.

Harvesting and using nasturtiums

Edible flowers and leaves

  • Flowers: Pick fresh, fully open blooms. They add colour and a peppery taste to salads.
  • Leaves: Young leaves are best for eating; use them like rocket or watercress.

Seeds

The green seeds can be pickled and used as a substitute for capers. Allow some flowers to set seed if you want self-sown plants next year or to collect seed for re-sowing.

Seasonal care and lifecycle

  • Spring: Sow seeds indoors or outdoors after frost; plant out young plants.
  • Summer: Peak flowering; keep plants tidy and water during dry spells.
  • Autumn: Plants will fade with colder nights; collect seeds if desired.

Nasturtiums are usually grown as annuals, but in very mild climates they may persist longer. In most gardens, you’ll re-sow each year or rely on self-seeding.

Quick reference

  • Type: Annual flower, edible leaves and blooms.
  • Light: Full sun to light shade.
  • Soil: Well-drained, not heavily fertilised.
  • Sowing depth: About 2 cm.
  • Spacing: 25–45 cm depending on variety.
  • Germination: 7–14 days in warm soil.
