How to Grow Lavender

A simple guide for fragrant, long-lasting Mediterranean herbs

About lavender

Lavender (Lavandula) is a hardy, drought-tolerant perennial known for its fragrant purple flowers and silvery foliage. It thrives in sunny, dry conditions and is perfect for borders, pots, wildlife gardens, and Mediterranean-style planting.

Choosing the right site

  • Light: Full sun — at least 6 hours daily.
  • Soil: Free-draining, sandy or gritty soil. Avoid heavy clay.
  • Drainage: Essential — lavender hates sitting in wet soil.
Tip: Add horticultural grit when planting to improve drainage.

Choosing varieties

  • English lavender: Hardy, long-lived, great for hedges.
  • French lavender: Showy bracts, earlier flowering, less hardy.
  • Dwarf varieties: Ideal for pots and small spaces.

Planting lavender

  1. Plant in spring or early summer.
  2. Dig a hole twice as wide as the rootball.
  3. Mix grit into the soil for drainage.
  4. Plant so the crown sits just above soil level.
  5. Water lightly to settle the soil.

Looking after lavender

Watering

  • New plants: Water regularly for the first 2–3 weeks.
  • Established plants: Only water during long dry spells.

Feeding

Lavender needs very little fertiliser — too much feed reduces fragrance and lifespan.

Pruning

  • Trim lightly after flowering to keep shape.
  • Avoid cutting into old woody stems.

Common problems

  • Leggy growth: Caused by lack of pruning or too much shade.
  • Root rot: Usually from poor drainage — improve soil or replant.

Harvesting and using lavender

  • Cut stems just before flowers fully open.
  • Use for drying, crafts, oils, or culinary purposes.

Quick reference

  • Type: Perennial herb.
  • Light: Full sun.
  • Soil: Free-draining, sandy.
  • Spacing: 30–45 cm.
  • Flowering: Summer.
