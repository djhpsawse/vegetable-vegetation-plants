Wildflowers to Grow and When to Grow Them

A simple, seasonal guide to sowing wildflowers so you can enjoy colour, pollinators, and a more natural garden all year long.

Why grow wildflowers?

Wildflowers are low‑maintenance, wildlife‑friendly, and perfect for adding natural beauty to borders, meadows, and even pots. By choosing the right mix and sowing at the right time, you can create a long‑lasting display that supports bees, butterflies, and other pollinators.

The best time to sow most wildflower seeds is either in spring or early autumn, when soil is moist and temperatures are mild. Below you’ll find ideas for what to grow and a simple calendar to keep you on track.

Best wildflowers to grow

Easy annual wildflowers

Annual wildflowers complete their life cycle in one year, but they flower quickly and generously. They’re ideal for new meadows, gaps in borders, or a quick burst of colour.

  • Cornflower – Classic blue flowers that bees love.
  • Common poppy – Bright red blooms that thrive in poor soil.
  • Corn marigold – Cheerful yellow flowers that mix well with poppies and cornflowers.
  • California poppy – Silky orange or yellow flowers that cope well with dry conditions.
  • Cosmos – Tall, airy plants with pink, white, or purple daisy‑like flowers.

Reliable perennial wildflowers

Perennial wildflowers take longer to establish but return year after year, gradually thickening into a stable meadow.

  • Oxeye daisy – Large white daisies that light up summer meadows.
  • Red campion – Pink flowers that do well in partial shade.
  • Yarrow – Flat flower heads in white or soft colours, great for pollinators.
  • Field scabious – Lilac pincushion flowers loved by bees and butterflies.
  • Bird’s‑foot trefoil – Low‑growing yellow flowers that support many caterpillars.

Shade‑tolerant wildflowers

If you have a shady corner, you can still grow wildflowers—just choose species that prefer less sun.

  • Bluebell – Ideal for woodland‑style areas (plant bulbs in autumn).
  • Wood anemone – Delicate white flowers for dappled shade.
  • Foxglove – Tall spires of bell‑shaped flowers, great for bees.
  • Primrose – Soft yellow flowers that appear in early spring.

When to sow wildflowers

Most wildflower mixes can be sown in either March–May or September–October. Spring sowings give you flowers in the same year (especially annuals), while autumn sowings establish strong roots for earlier flowering the following year.

Spring sowing March–May

As the soil warms, seeds germinate quickly. This is a great time for annual mixes and areas that get full sun.

  • Best for: Cornflowers, poppies, corn marigold, cosmos.
  • Soil: Rake to a fine tilth and remove weeds.
  • Depth: Scatter on the surface and lightly rake in.
  • Watering: Keep moist until seedlings are established.

Autumn sowing September–October

Cooler temperatures and autumn rain help seeds settle and root before winter, especially perennials and meadow mixes.

  • Best for: Oxeye daisy, yarrow, red campion, meadow mixes.
  • Soil: Clear grass and weeds; wildflowers prefer low‑fertility soil.
  • Frost: Many species benefit from winter cold to break dormancy.
  • Result: Stronger plants and earlier flowers next year.

Bulbs and plugs Autumn & early spring

Some wildflowers are easier to establish from bulbs or small plug plants rather than seed.

  • Bluebells & primroses: Plant in autumn for spring flowers.
  • Foxgloves: Plant young plants in spring or autumn.
  • Shady spots: Tuck bulbs and plugs into leaf litter or under shrubs.
Tip: In very cold or very wet conditions, wait for a dry, mild spell before sowing. Seeds need contact with soil and some warmth to germinate well.

Simple wildflower sowing calendar

Use this rough calendar as a guide. Exact timing will depend on your local climate and soil conditions.

Month What to do Good choices
February Plan your meadow, clear weeds, and prepare soil. Order seed mixes and bulbs.
March Start spring sowing in milder areas. Cornflower, poppy, corn marigold, annual mixes.
April Main spring sowing period; keep soil moist. Most annual wildflowers, some perennials.
May Last chance for spring sowing in cooler areas. Fast‑growing annuals like cornflower and cosmos.
June–August Enjoy flowers; mow or cut back once seeds have dropped. All established wildflowers.
September Begin autumn sowing; prepare new areas. Perennial mixes, meadow blends, oxeye daisy, yarrow.
October Finish autumn sowing before heavy frosts. Perennials and hardy annuals that like a cold spell.
November–January Rest period; avoid sowing, but you can plant some bulbs. Bluebells, wild daffodils, other woodland bulbs.

Looking after your wildflower patch

Once your wildflowers are growing, they need surprisingly little care. Avoid feeding the soil, as rich ground encourages coarse grasses and weeds rather than flowers.

  • Watering: Only in very dry spells, especially in the first year.
  • Weeds: Hand‑pull obvious weeds before they set seed.
  • Mowing: For meadows, cut once or twice a year after flowering and remove the cuttings.
  • Reseeding: Scatter a little fresh seed each year if you want to keep lots of annuals.
Wildlife bonus: Leave some seed heads standing over winter. They provide food for birds and shelter for insects.
